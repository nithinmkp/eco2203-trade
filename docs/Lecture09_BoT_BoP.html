Balance of Trade & Balance of Payments

Following the Money: India’s External Sector

Introduction: Why the BoP Matters

Trade policy affects the current account. Exchange rate policy affects the financial account. Both show up in the Balance of Payments (BoP).

Key questions for today:

  • How does a country “pay” for its imports?
  • What happens when a country consistently imports more than it exports?
  • How do capital flows balance the external accounts?
  • When does a BoP crisis occur?

India’s external sector at a glance (FY2024):

  • Merchandise exports: $437 billion (goods)
  • Merchandise imports: $677 billion (goods)
  • Trade deficit: -$240 billion (goods only)
  • Services surplus: +$163B
  • Remittances: +$120B (world’s largest recipient)
  • Current account: -$23B (-0.7% of GDP)

BoP accounting identity:

\[\underbrace{\text{CA}}_{\text{trade + income}} + \underbrace{\text{KA}}_{\text{transfers}} + \underbrace{\text{FA}}_{\text{capital flows}} + \underbrace{\Delta R}_{\text{reserve change}} = 0\]

The BoP always balances — by accounting construction.

A current account deficit must be financed by: 1. Capital/financial inflows (borrowing, FDI, FPI), or 2. Running down foreign exchange reserves

If neither is available → BoP crisis (India 1991).

Balance of Trade: The Basics

Balance of Trade (BoT) = Merchandise exports − Merchandise imports

\[\text{BoT} = X_{\text{goods}} - M_{\text{goods}}\]

  • Positive BoT = trade surplus (exports > imports)
  • Negative BoT = trade deficit (imports > exports)

India’s BoT trend (USD billion):

India always runs a merchandise trade deficit — driven by oil, gold, and electronics imports.

Gold imports: 700–800 tonnes/year → cultural and investment demand.

National Income Identity Approach to the Current Account

National Income and Product Accounts (NIPA):

\[Y = C + I + G + (X - M)\]

Rearranging:

\[X - M = Y - (C + I + G) = Y - A\]

where \(A = C + I + G\) is absorption (total domestic spending).

Implication:

\[\boxed{\text{Current Account} \approx Y - A}\]

  • \(\text{CA} > 0 \Leftrightarrow Y > A\): country produces more than it spends → net lender to world
  • \(\text{CA} < 0 \Leftrightarrow Y < A\): country spends more than it produces → net borrower from world

Saving-investment identity:

\[Y - A = Y - C - I - G = (Y - C - G) - I = S_{\text{private}} + S_{\text{govt}} - I\]

\[\boxed{\text{CA} = (S - I) + (T - G)}\]

CA deficit = private dis-saving OR investment boom OR government deficit (or combination).

India’s CA decomposition (FY2024):

\[\text{CA} = (S - I) + (T - G)\]

  • Private saving rate: ~31% of GDP
  • Investment rate: ~32% of GDP → private \(S - I \approx -1\%\) GDP
  • Fiscal deficit: ~5.1% of GDP → \(T - G \approx -5.1\%\)
  • CA = \(-1\% + (-5.1\%) \approx -6\%\)?

But India’s CA = only \(-0.7\%\) GDP!

Why? Remittances ($120B) and services surplus ($163B) add back ~9-10% of GDP in non-merchandise credits.

Lesson: CA = S-I identity holds, but “saving” must include remittance income and service exports.

Current and Capital Account Structure

Account 1: Current Account (CA)

  • Merchandise trade (goods): exports (+), imports (-)
  • Services trade: software, tourism, BPO
  • Primary income: investment income (interest, dividends, profits) — net
  • Secondary income: remittances, grants, transfers

Account 2: Capital Account (KA)

  • Capital transfers (debt forgiveness, migrant assets)
  • Acquisition/disposal of non-produced, non-financial assets
  • (Small for most countries including India)

Accounting rule:

Every international transaction has two entries of equal and opposite sign (double-entry bookkeeping):

Example: India imports $10M of machines:

  • Current Account (imports): \(-10M\)
  • Financial Account (credit from foreign bank): \(+10M\)

Example: Indian software firm receives $5M:

  • Current Account (services export): \(+5M\)
  • Financial Account (forex held abroad initially): \(-5M\)

Financial Account and BoP Identity

Account 3: Financial Account (FA)

  • Foreign Direct Investment (FDI): equity + reinvested earnings
  • Portfolio Investment (FPI/FII): equities, bonds
  • Other investment: loans, trade credit, bank deposits
  • Official reserves (RBI intervention)

BoP identity:

\[\text{CA} + \text{KA} + \text{FA} + \Delta R = 0\]

The BoP always balances — by accounting construction. A current account deficit must be financed by:

  1. Capital/financial inflows (borrowing, FDI, FPI), or
  2. Running down foreign exchange reserves

If neither is available → BoP crisis (India 1991).

India’s BoP summary (FY2024):

Component USD B
Current Account −23
Financial Account +20
Δ Reserves +3
Total ≈ 0

India’s current account deficit of ~$23B is financed primarily through FDI ($22B) and FPI ($41B) inflows — making financial account management critical to external stability.

Official Reserves and RBI Intervention

Official Foreign Exchange Reserves = foreign assets held by central bank (RBI):

  • Foreign currency assets (mainly USD, EUR, GBP, JPY)
  • Gold
  • SDRs (IMF Special Drawing Rights)
  • Reserve tranche position at IMF

India’s reserves trajectory:

Year Reserves (USD B) Import cover
FY1991 1.2 2 weeks
FY2000 38 8 months
FY2008 309 14 months
FY2020 477 12 months
FY2024 646 11 months

Adequacy benchmark: 3 months of imports (IMF rule of thumb). India holds >11 months.

RBI’s intervention role:

RBI buys/sells USD to manage the exchange rate:

  • Buy USD (sell INR) → prevents excessive appreciation, builds reserves
  • Sell USD (buy INR) → prevents excessive depreciation, depletes reserves

Managed float: India operates a “managed float” — neither fully fixed nor fully free.

RBI intervenes to: 1. Smooth volatility (not set a target level) 2. Prevent disorderly market conditions 3. Maintain competitive export pricing (keep INR from appreciating too fast)

FY2024: RBI was a net buyer of USD — absorbing strong capital inflows.

BoP Adjustment Mechanisms

When a BoP deficit exists, three adjustment mechanisms operate:

1. Price mechanism (exchange rate adjustment):

Currency depreciates → exports cheaper, imports expensive → CA improves (if M-L condition holds).

2. Income/expenditure mechanism:

CA deficit → domestic income falls (through multiplier) → absorption \(A\) falls → CA improves.

\[\Delta M = m \cdot \Delta Y \quad \text{(marginal propensity to import)}\]

3. Monetary mechanism:

CA deficit → reserve outflow → money supply falls → domestic prices fall → exports competitive again (Hume’s price-specie-flow mechanism).

Modern version: reserve loss tightens monetary conditions → interest rates rise → capital inflows.

Policy instruments:

Problem Instrument Channel
CA deficit Devaluation Price competitiveness
CA deficit Expenditure reduction Income mechanism
FA deficit Interest rate rise Capital inflows
Reserve crisis IMF credit Financing

Expenditure-switching vs Expenditure-reducing:

  • Switching: devaluation shifts demand from foreign to domestic goods
  • Reducing: fiscal/monetary austerity reduces total absorption

Both often needed simultaneously — as India learned in 1991.

Marshall-Lerner Condition: When Does Depreciation Help?

Question: Does a currency depreciation always improve the trade balance?

Answer: Only if demand is elastic enough.

Derivation:

Let exchange rate \(e\) = domestic currency per unit of foreign. Depreciation means \(\Delta e > 0\).

Trade balance in domestic currency:

\[TB = P_X \cdot Q_X - e \cdot P_M^* \cdot Q_M\]

where \(P_M^*\) = foreign price of imports, \(Q_X, Q_M\) = quantities.

After depreciation, \(e \uparrow\): - Export price (foreign) falls → \(Q_X \uparrow\) by \(|e_X| \cdot \frac{\Delta e}{e}\) - Import price (domestic) rises → \(Q_M \downarrow\) by \(|e_M| \cdot \frac{\Delta e}{e}\)

Marshall-Lerner (M-L) condition:

\[\boxed{|e_X| + |e_M| > 1}\]

If satisfied, depreciation improves the trade balance. If \(|e_X| + |e_M| < 1\), depreciation worsens TB (short-run typical).

Intuition:

A 10% depreciation:

  • Makes exports 10% cheaper abroad → quantity effect (positive)
  • Makes imports 10% expensive at home → quantity effect (positive for TB)
  • But also raises value of existing imports → value effect (negative for TB)

M-L says: quantity effects must outweigh value effect.

Typical elasticity estimates (long-run):

\(|e_X|\) \(|e_M|\) Sum M-L satisfied?
India 0.8–1.2 0.7–1.0 ~1.5–2.2
Short-run 0.3–0.5 0.2–0.4 ~0.5–0.9

Short-run inelasticity: contracts pre-set, adjustment takes time.

J-Curve: Mechanisms

Why does TB worsen initially?

Phase I (0–3 months): Contracts and inelasticity

  • Import/export contracts already written in foreign currency
  • Prices change, but quantities cannot adjust instantly
  • Rising import prices → import bill rises (value effect > quantity effect)
  • M-L condition NOT satisfied in short run: \(|e_X^{SR}| + |e_M^{SR}| < 1\)

Phase II (3–12 months): Gradual adjustment

  • Firms begin adjusting: export more, seek import substitutes
  • Trade volumes start responding to price signals

Phase III (12+ months): Full adjustment

  • Contracts renegotiated
  • Production capacity adjusted
  • Consumers switch to domestic substitutes
  • M-L holds: \(|e_X^{LR}| + |e_M^{LR}| > 1\)

India’s empirical evidence:

INR depreciation episodes: - 1991: 25% devaluation → J-curve lasted ~2 years (severe structural rigidities) - 2013 taper tantrum: INR fell 20% → TB improved within 12 months (better-integrated trade) - 2022 (post-Russia-Ukraine): INR weakened 10% → limited TB improvement (oil price dominated)

Agricultural trade J-curve:

Agricultural exports show faster adjustment than industrial goods — lower contract lock-in for spot commodity trades.

But Indian farmers often price in INR and don’t automatically capture depreciation gains without better market linkages.

Absorption Approach: Policy Implications

Absorption approach (Sidney Alexander, 1952):

\[CA = Y - A\]

To improve the CA (reduce deficit), need:

\[\Delta CA = \Delta Y - \Delta A > 0\]

Either: 1. Raise output \(Y\): supply-side reforms, productivity growth 2. Reduce absorption \(A\): cut \(C\), \(I\), or \(G\) (expenditure reduction) 3. Both (typical IMF conditionality)

Devaluation alone is insufficient:

If \(A\) rises as fast as \(Y\) (say, because higher export revenues are spent on imports), CA does not improve.

Sterilisation problem: If devaluation raises domestic price level (inflation), real \(A\) may not fall → M-L gains are inflated away.

India 1991 adjustment:

\[\Delta CA = \Delta Y - \Delta A\]

IMF conditionality required: 1. Fiscal deficit cut: \(\Delta G < 0\) → reduces \(A\) 2. 25% devaluation → switches \(A\) toward domestic goods 3. Trade liberalisation → improves efficiency of \(Y\)

Result: - CA went from -3.1% GDP (FY91) to -1.0% (FY93) - Growth fell temporarily (-0.1% in FY91-92) - Inflation spiked (13.7% in FY92)

Classic adjustment tradeoff: external balance restored at cost of internal balance — at least temporarily.

India’s 1991 BoP Crisis

Chronology of the crisis:

Date Event
1988–90 Gulf remittances fall (Gulf War I); oil prices spike
1990 Fiscal deficit ~8.5% GDP; political instability
Oct 1990 Moody’s downgrades India
Jan 1991 Non-resident Indian deposits withdrawn en masse
Mar 1991 Forex reserves: $5.8B (barely 3 weeks imports)
June 1991 Reserves: $1.2B — 2 WEEKS of imports
July 1991 India pledges 67 tonnes of gold to Bank of England + Bank of Japan for $600M loan
Aug 1991 IMF Structural Adjustment Package: $2.2B
1991–92 25% devaluation; trade and investment liberalisation

Root causes:

  1. Fiscal profligacy: 8.5% fiscal deficit financed by short-term external borrowing
  2. Oil shock (1990): Iraq invasion of Kuwait → oil price doubled
  3. Remittance shock: NRI deposits withdrawn on political uncertainty
  4. Structural rigidities: ISI (import substitution) model had exhausted itself

IMF conditionality:

  • Fiscal deficit reduction
  • Trade liberalisation: import licensing abolished
  • Industrial delicensing (1991 Industrial Policy)
  • FDI liberalisation: automatic route up to 51%

Legacy: The 1991 crisis was the proximate cause of India’s liberalisation — turning a crisis into a structural reform.

India FY2024 BoP Assessment

Current Account:

  • CA deficit: $23.2B (-0.7% of GDP) — comfortable
  • Merchandise trade deficit: $238B (oil, gold, electronics)
  • Services surplus: $163B (IT/BPO dominance)
  • Remittances: $120B — world’s largest recipient (World Bank data)

Financial Account:

  • FDI net inflows: $22B (declined from $83B peak in FY22)
  • FPI: +$41B (strong equity market inflows)
  • ECB + banking capital: +$25B

Reserve adequacy:

  • Forex reserves: $646B (March 2024)
  • Import cover: ~11 months (well above IMF 3-month threshold)
  • Short-term debt/reserves: 19% (well below 100% danger threshold)

Risks and concerns:

  1. Oil price vulnerability: $10/barrel rise adds ~$15B to import bill
  2. Gold imports: Cultural demand makes gold import compression difficult
  3. FPI volatility: $41B inflow in FY24; could reverse quickly (as in 2013 taper tantrum)
  4. FDI decline: Net FDI fell from $55B (FY22) to $22B (FY24) — concerning for long-run financing
  5. China factor: RCEP non-membership means India lacks preferential access; competition from China in exports

Sustainable CA deficit?

\[\text{CA/GDP} < \text{long-run growth rate} \times \frac{\text{Net foreign debt}}{\text{GDP}}\]

India’s CA/GDP (-0.7%) << GDP growth (7%) — external position is sustainable and comfortable.

BoP and Agricultural Policy Linkages

How agricultural policy affects BoP:

  1. Food import substitution: MSP → higher domestic production → reduces food import bill

    • India became self-sufficient in pulses (FY2023) — saved ~$3B/year
    • Oilseed: still imports ~$22B of edible oils despite MSP

  2. Export competitiveness: High MSP + input subsidies → high domestic costs → reduces agricultural export competitiveness

  3. Rice exports: Restrictions in 2023 saved BoP in short run (reduced export earnings but improved food security), hurt long-run reputation

  4. Remittance multiplier: 5-6M agricultural workers in Gulf + Western countries send ~$40-50B/year from agri-related labour → largest single CA credit item after IT services

WTO Mode 3 & 4 negotiations: India pushes for freer movement of skilled professionals (Mode 4) — a key interest in services trade negotiations.

Channels:

  1. Consumption smoothing: farm households use remittances during crop failure
  2. Investment: farm equipment, irrigation, land purchases
  3. Risk-taking: remittance income allows shift to higher-value crops
  4. Labour market: outmigration raises rural wages → pushes up agricultural labour costs

BoP significance:

Remittances = $120B vs FDI = $22B (FY24).

India is less dependent on volatile FDI/FPI for BoP balance than most large EMs — remittances provide stable “private foreign aid.”

Key Takeaways: Lecture 9

1. National income identity: \(\text{CA} = Y - A = (S - I) + (T - G)\). A CA deficit means the country borrows from abroad — financed by FA inflows or reserve drawdown.

2. BoP accounting: \(\text{CA} + \text{KA} + \text{FA} + \Delta R = 0\). The BoP always balances — surpluses and deficits are in individual accounts, not the overall BoP.

3. India’s external position (FY2024): CA = -0.7% GDP (comfortable); remittances = $120B (world’s largest); services surplus = $163B; forex reserves = $646B (11 months imports). External position is strong.

4. Marshall-Lerner condition: \(|e_X| + |e_M| > 1\) must hold for depreciation to improve TB. In the short run, M-L is typically NOT satisfied → J-curve: TB worsens before improving.

5. India 1991 crisis: Reserves fell to $1.2B (2 weeks imports); India pledged 67 tonnes gold to the BoE. IMF bailout triggered liberalisation — delicensing, trade reform, FDI opening. Crisis → structural reform.

6. Agricultural BoP: India runs a trade surplus in agriculture (~$20B), but edible oil imports (-$21B) are the single largest structural weakness. MSP policy affects both food security and export competitiveness.
