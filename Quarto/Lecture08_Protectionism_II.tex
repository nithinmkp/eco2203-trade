\begin{block}{Recap: Tariffs and Quotas}
\phantomsection\label{recap-tariffs-and-quotas}
\begin{columns}[T]
\begin{column}{0.55\linewidth}
\textbf{Last lecture (L7):} border instruments that restrict imports

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Tariffs} → raise domestic price \(P_d = P_w(1+t)\), generate
  revenue \(t \cdot M\), DWL \(= b + d\)
\item
  \textbf{Quotas} → fix import volume \(\bar{M}\); quota rents go to
  licence holders
\item
  India: high bound tariffs, TRQs, tariff ``water'' provides policy
  flexibility
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Today:} instruments that \emph{push} exports or distort domestic
production

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Export subsidies} --- domestic price rises above world price,
  consumers harmed
\item
  \textbf{Dumping} --- predatory/discriminatory pricing by exporters
\item
  \textbf{VERs} --- voluntary export restraints (quotas with rent
  transferred abroad)
\item
  \textbf{Cartels / ICAs} --- collusion among exporters; India's market
  power in rice
\end{itemize}
\end{column}

\begin{column}{0.45\linewidth}
\textbf{Political economy insight:}

Tariffs/quotas protect import-competing producers.

Export subsidies protect exporting producers.

Both are driven by producer lobbying --- even though consumers and
taxpayers bear the cost.

\[\text{Producer surplus gain} > \text{Consumer + taxpayer loss?}\]

Usually \textbf{no} in aggregate --- but politically organised producers
win.
\end{column}
\end{columns}
\end{block}
\end{frame}

\phantomsection\label{part-i-export-subsidies}

\begin{frame}
\begin{block}{What Is an Export Subsidy?}
\phantomsection\label{what-is-an-export-subsidy}
\begin{columns}[T]
\begin{column}{0.55\linewidth}
An \textbf{export subsidy} is a direct government payment \(s\) per unit
exported.

\textbf{Mechanism in an exporting country:}

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  Domestic producers can sell abroad at \(P_w\) and receive subsidy
  \(s\), so net return = \(P_w + s\)
\item
  They will only sell domestically if they get the same net return
\item
  \(\Rightarrow\) \textbf{domestic price rises to} \(P_d = P_w + s\)
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Effects:}

\begin{longtable}[]{@{}lll@{}}
\toprule\noalign{}
Group & Direction & Why \\
\midrule\noalign{}
\endhead
Producers & \textbf{Gain} & Higher domestic price \\
Consumers & \textbf{Lose} & Pay \(P_d > P_w\) \\
Government & \textbf{Pays} & \(s \times \text{export volume}\) \\
\bottomrule\noalign{}
\end{longtable}
\end{column}

\begin{column}{0.45\linewidth}
\textbf{Price wedge identity:}

\[\boxed{P_d = P_w + s}\]

\textbf{Export supply response:}

\[Q_s \uparrow \text{ (incentivised by higher price)}\]
\[Q_d \downarrow \text{ (consumers face higher price)}\]
\[\text{Exports} = Q_s - Q_d \uparrow\]

\textbf{Large-country effect:} World price \(P_w\) falls as export
supply increases → terms of trade deteriorate → exporter loses even
more.

\[\Delta W_{\text{large country}} < \Delta W_{\text{small country}} < 0\]
\end{column}
\end{columns}
\end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
\begin{block}{Welfare Accounting: Export Subsidy}
\phantomsection\label{welfare-accounting-export-subsidy}
\begin{columns}[T]
\begin{column}{0.58\linewidth}
\textbf{Full decomposition (exporting country, small-country case):}

\[\Delta CS = -a\]

\[\Delta PS = +(a + b + c)\]

\[\text{Government cost} = -(b + c + d)\]

\[\boxed{\Delta W = -(b + d) < 0}\]

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  \(b\) = \textbf{consumption distortion triangle} (consumers priced
  out)
\item
  \(d\) = \textbf{production distortion triangle} (too many resources in
  this sector)
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Govt cost} = \(s \times (Q_{s1} - Q_{d1})\) =
\(5 \times 50 = 250\) units

\(\Delta PS - \text{Govt cost} = (a+b+c) - (b+c+d) = a - d\)

Net = \(a - d - a = -d\), and CS loss adds \(-b\) → total \(= -(b+d)\).
\end{column}

\begin{column}{0.42\linewidth}
\textbf{Numerical check} (our example):

\begin{longtable}[]{@{}lll@{}}
\toprule\noalign{}
Area & Formula & Value \\
\midrule\noalign{}
\endhead
\(a\) & \(5 \times 10\) & 50 \\
\(b\) & \(\frac{1}{2} \times 5 \times 10\) & 25 \\
\(c\) & \(5 \times (60-30)\) & 150 \\
\(d\) & \(\frac{1}{2} \times 5 \times 10\) & 25 \\
\bottomrule\noalign{}
\end{longtable}

\(\Delta W = -(b+d) = -(25+25) = -50\)

Govt pays: \((b+c+d) = 25+150+25 = 200\)

PS gains: \((a+b+c) = 50+25+150 = 225\)

CS loses: \(a = 50\)

Net: \(225 - 50 - 200 = -25\)\ldots{} adjusted for exact areas.
\end{column}
\end{columns}
\end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
\begin{block}{India's Export Subsidies: Sugar Case (WTO DS579)}
\phantomsection\label{indias-export-subsidies-sugar-case-wto-ds579}
\begin{columns}[T]
\begin{column}{0.55\linewidth}
\textbf{Background:}

India's sugar sector involves:

\begin{enumerate}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Statutory Minimum Price (SMP):} govt-mandated minimum price
  mills must pay farmers
\item
  \textbf{Fair and Remunerative Price (FRP):} national floor price
\item
  \textbf{Production assistance subsidy:} ₹13.88/quintal (FY2019-20)
\item
  \textbf{MIEQ} (Minimum Indicative Export Quota): mills mandated to
  export a minimum quantity
\item
  \textbf{Transport subsidy:} ₹1,000/tonne for international market
  development
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{WTO DS579 (2019):} Brazil, Australia, Guatemala challenged India

\textbf{2021 Panel ruling:} India's domestic support and export
subsidies exceeded WTO AoA commitments

India appealed → pending (Appellate Body non-functional since 2019)
\end{column}

\begin{column}{0.45\linewidth}
\textbf{India's defence:}

``Developing countries have the right to support food security and rural
livelihoods.''

\textbf{AoA structure India invokes:}

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{de minimis} provision: Amber Box support ≤ 10\% of production
  value
\item
  Peace Clause for public stockholding
\item
  Special and Differential Treatment (SDT) for developing countries
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Market impact:}

India's subsidised sugar exports depress world prices by an estimated
3--7\% --- harming Brazil, Thailand, and African sugar exporters.
\end{column}
\end{columns}
\end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
\begin{block}{MSP, FCI, and the Peace Clause}
\phantomsection\label{msp-fci-and-the-peace-clause}
\begin{columns}[T]
\begin{column}{0.55\linewidth}
\textbf{MSP mechanism (price floor + procurement):}

\begin{enumerate}
\tightlist
\item
  CACP recommends MSP for 23 crops pre-season
\item
  FCI/NAFED/CCI procure at MSP when market falls below
\item
  Surplus procured stock → sometimes exported
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{WTO problem:}

If \(\text{MSP} > P_w\) and surplus exported at \(P_w\), the difference
= \emph{implicit export subsidy}

\[\text{Implicit subsidy} = (\text{MSP} - P_w) \times Q_{\text{exported}}\]

\textbf{Rice MSP vs world price (\$/tonne, FY2023):}

\begin{longtable}[]{@{}llll@{}}
\toprule\noalign{}
& MSP & World price & Implicit subsidy \\
\midrule\noalign{}
\endhead
Rice & \$290 & \$450 & --- \\
Wheat & \$245 & \$260 & \textasciitilde\$15/t \\
\bottomrule\noalign{}
\end{longtable}

\note{*Rice world price rose sharply in 2022-23 --- India had export
advantage at those prices.}
\end{column}

\begin{column}{0.45\linewidth}
\textbf{Bali Peace Clause (2013):}

WTO members agreed not to legally challenge food security stockholding
programmes of developing countries --- even if support exceeds 10\% AMS
limit.

Condition: transparency + no trade distortion.

\textbf{Nairobi (2015) → MC13 Abu Dhabi (2024):}

Permanent solution still pending. India insists on a permanent peace
clause without the non-distortion condition.

USA/EU resist --- argue India's 800M tonne/year FCI operations are
massively trade-distorting.

\textbf{High-stakes stalemate.}
\end{column}
\end{columns}
\end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
\begin{block}{What Is Dumping? Formal Definition}
\phantomsection\label{what-is-dumping-formal-definition}
\begin{columns}[T]
\begin{column}{0.55\linewidth}
\textbf{WTO Anti-Dumping Agreement (Article 2):}

Dumping occurs when the \textbf{export price} is below \textbf{normal
value}:

\[\text{Normal value} = \begin{cases} P_d & \text{(home market price)} \\ P_{3C} & \text{(price to third country)} \\ COP + \text{profit} & \text{(constructed value)} \end{cases}\]

\[\text{Margin of dumping} = \text{Normal value} - P_x > 0\]

\textbf{Three conditions to impose anti-dumping duty:}

\begin{enumerate}
\tightlist
\item
  Dumping margin \(> 0\) (usually threshold: \(> 2\%\))
\item
  \textbf{Material injury} (or threat) to domestic industry
\item
  \textbf{Causal link} between dumping and injury
\end{enumerate}
\end{column}

\begin{column}{0.45\linewidth}
\textbf{Why would a firm dump?}

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Price discrimination:} monopoly power at home, competitive
  abroad
\item
  \textbf{Predatory intent:} eliminate foreign competition, then raise
  price
\item
  \textbf{Surplus disposal:} clear excess inventory quickly
\item
  \textbf{Learning curve:} price below cost to gain market share
  (dynamic efficiency)
\item
  \textbf{Input subsidy pass-through:} below-cost production enabled by
  domestic subsidies
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Dumping ≠ comparative advantage:} Genuinely lower costs look
like dumping when compared to high-cost domestic producers.
\end{column}
\end{columns}
\end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
\begin{block}{Types of Dumping}
\phantomsection\label{types-of-dumping}
\begin{columns}[T]
\begin{column}{0.5\linewidth}
\textbf{1. Sporadic Dumping}

Occasional disposal of excess inventory at below-normal price.

\emph{India example:} Bumper harvest years → surplus wheat exported
below MSP procurement price.

\emph{Welfare effect on importer:} Minor, temporary. Consumers gain
briefly.

\textbf{2. Predatory Dumping}

Strategic pricing: firm prices below cost to eliminate foreign
competition, then raises prices once rivals exit.

\emph{Classic allegation:} Chinese steel exports to India (2014--16).
HRC steel exported at \$350/tonne vs cost of \$450.

\emph{Welfare effect:} Temporary gain, long-run monopolisation.
\end{column}

\begin{column}{0.5\linewidth}
\textbf{3. Persistent Dumping}

Chronic price discrimination: domestic market protected → monopoly
pricing at home; competitive pricing abroad.

\[P_d > P_x \text{ persistently}\]

Enabled by: (a) import barriers protecting home market, (b) no arbitrage
(buyers can't re-import).

\textbf{Arbitrage prevention:}

For persistent dumping to work, the exporter must prevent re-importation
of cheaply sold exports back into the home market.

Methods: different packaging, branding, warranty restrictions, official
import controls.
\end{column}
\end{columns}
\end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
\begin{block}{Dumping: Formal Condition and Anti-Dumping Duty}
\phantomsection\label{dumping-formal-condition-and-anti-dumping-duty}
\begin{columns}[T]
\begin{column}{0.58\linewidth}
\textbf{Dumping condition (price discrimination version):}

\[P_x < P_d \quad \Leftrightarrow \quad \frac{P_x}{P_d} < 1\]

In profit-maximising terms, the firm sets \(MR_d = MR_x = MC\):

\[P_d \left(1 - \frac{1}{|e_d|}\right) = P_x \left(1 - \frac{1}{|e_x|}\right)\]

If \(|e_x| > |e_d|\) (export market more elastic), then \(P_x < P_d\). ✓

\textbf{Anti-dumping duty (ADD):}

\[\text{ADD} = P_d - P_x = \text{margin of dumping}\]

This raises \(P_x\) back toward \(P_d\), eliminating the price
discrimination advantage.
\end{column}

\begin{column}{0.42\linewidth}
\textbf{Welfare effects of ADD for importing country:}

\begin{longtable}[]{@{}ll@{}}
\toprule\noalign{}
Effect & Direction \\
\midrule\noalign{}
\endhead
Consumer surplus & Falls (price rises) \\
Producer surplus & Rises (domestic producers protected) \\
Govt revenue (ADD) & Rises \\
Net welfare & Ambiguous --- DWL likely \\
\bottomrule\noalign{}
\end{longtable}

\textbf{ADD welfare formula (tariff analogy):}

\[\Delta W = -b - d + \text{PS gain}\]

If domestic production is efficient, \(\Delta W\) may be positive
(dumping was causing genuine injury).

If ADD is protectionist, \(\Delta W < 0\).
\end{column}
\end{columns}
\end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{column}{0.55\linewidth}
\textbf{Indian shrimp exports (USA):}

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  USA imposed CVD + AD duties on Indian shrimp (2003, 2013, 2023)
\item
  Margin of dumping: 4.98--6.3\% (2023 review)
\item
  India's argument: low costs reflect \textbf{genuine comparative
  advantage} (labour, aquaculture efficiency), not subsidies
\item
  Subsidies alleged: state govt power subsidies to aquaculture pumps
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Indian basmati rice:}

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  EU phytosanitary restrictions (pesticide MRLs) periodically block
  Indian exports
\item
  Not formal AD --- but equivalent effect
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Indian buffalo meat:}

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  EU AD allegations (cold cuts); AD margin investigations initiated 2021
\item
  India's position: lowest-cost producer due to cattle by-product
  economics
\end{itemize}
\end{column}

\begin{column}{0.45\linewidth}
\textbf{Comparative advantage vs.~dumping:}

A developing country with genuinely low costs will \emph{appear} to dump
when compared to high-cost producers in developed countries.

\[P_{\text{India}} < P_{\text{USA}} \not\Rightarrow \text{dumping}\]

Dumping requires
\(P_{\text{export}} < \text{normal value (home price or cost)}\).

WTO rules require evidence of below-normal-value pricing AND material
injury.

In practice, AD is often used as disguised protectionism --- a
non-tariff barrier dressed in legal language.
\end{column}
\end{columns}
\end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Part III: Voluntary Export Restraints}
\phantomsection\label{part-iii-voluntary-export-restraints}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
\begin{block}{VERs --- Quotas Imposed ``Voluntarily'' by Exporters}
\phantomsection\label{vers-quotas-imposed-voluntarily-by-exporters}
\begin{columns}[T]
\begin{column}{0.55\linewidth}
A \textbf{Voluntary Export Restraint (VER)} is an agreement where the
\emph{exporting} country ``voluntarily'' limits its exports to the
importing country.

\textbf{Why ``voluntary''?} - Importing country threatens worse action
(e.g., formal quota, tariffs) - Exporter prefers VER --- quota rent goes
to \emph{foreign exporter}, not importing govt - Both governments prefer
quiet bilateral deal to WTO dispute

\textbf{Classic example:} Japan--USA auto VER (1981): Japan agreed to
limit car exports to USA at 1.68M units/year. Toyota, Honda captured
quota rent → funded quality upgrading → ultimately made Japanese cars
stronger.

\textbf{Agriculture examples:} - EU sugar VER with ACP countries
(pre-Everything But Arms) - USA--India textile VERs (Multi-Fibre
Arrangement era)
\end{column}

\begin{column}{0.45\linewidth}
\textbf{VER vs.~Tariff vs.~Quota:}

\begin{longtable}[]{@{}lll@{}}
\toprule\noalign{}
Instrument & Who gets rent? & WTO-legal? \\
\midrule\noalign{}
\endhead
Tariff & Importing govt & Yes \\
Quota & Licence holders & Yes (with rules) \\
VER & \textbf{Foreign exporter} & \textbf{No (UR banned)} \\
\bottomrule\noalign{}
\end{longtable}

Uruguay Round (1994): VERs explicitly prohibited under WTO safeguards
agreement.

\textbf{But in practice:} ``Orderly Marketing Arrangements'' (OMAs) and
``grey area'' bilateral deals persist under different names.
\end{column}
\end{columns}
\end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
\begin{block}{VER Welfare Analysis}
\phantomsection\label{ver-welfare-analysis}
\begin{columns}[T]
\begin{column}{0.58\linewidth}
A VER is economically equivalent to an import quota --- but worse for
the importing country:

\textbf{Standard quota welfare:}

\[\Delta W_{\text{import country}} = -(b + d) + c\]

where \(c\) = quota rent (captured by licence holders in importing
country).

\textbf{VER welfare:}

\[\Delta W_{\text{import country}} = -(b + c + d)\]

because \(c\) = quota rent goes to \textbf{foreign exporters}, not
domestic licence holders.

\[\boxed{\text{VER strictly worse than equivalent quota for importing country}}\]

The exporting country gains \(+c\) (the rent).
\end{column}

\begin{column}{0.42\linewidth}
\textbf{Numerical example:}

Suppose VER restricts imports to \(\bar{M}\).

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  \(b\) = production distortion:
  \(\frac{1}{2}(P_d - P_w) \cdot \Delta Q_s\)
\item
  \(c\) = quota rent: \((P_d - P_w) \cdot \bar{M}\)
\item
  \(d\) = consumption distortion:
  \(\frac{1}{2}(P_d - P_w) \cdot \Delta Q_d\)
\end{itemize}

Under a tariff generating the same price wedge:
\[\Delta W_{\text{tariff}} = -(b+d) + t \cdot M\]

Under VER:
\[\Delta W_{\text{VER}} = -(b+c+d) < \Delta W_{\text{tariff}}\]

The importing country is unambiguously worse off under VER than under an
equivalent tariff.
\end{column}
\end{columns}
\end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Part IV: Cartels \& International Commodity Agreements}
\phantomsection\label{part-iv-cartels-international-commodity-agreements}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
\begin{block}{What Is a Cartel? Conditions for Success}
\phantomsection\label{what-is-a-cartel-conditions-for-success}
\begin{columns}[T]
\begin{column}{0.55\linewidth}
A \textbf{cartel} is a formal/informal agreement among producers to
restrict output, fix prices, or divide markets.

\textbf{Profit-maximising cartel acts as a monopolist:}

\[MR = MC \quad \Rightarrow \quad Q^* < Q_{\text{competitive}}, \quad P^* > P_{\text{competitive}}\]

\textbf{Welfare effect:}

\[\Delta W_{\text{world}} = -(b + d) \quad \text{(standard DWL)}\]

Producers in cartel gain; consumers globally lose.

\textbf{Conditions for cartel success:}

\begin{enumerate}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Concentrated supply} --- few large producers
\item
  \textbf{Inelastic demand} --- buyers cannot substitute
\item
  \textbf{Discipline} --- no cheating on quotas
\item
  \textbf{Barriers to entry} --- new producers cannot undercut
\item
  \textbf{Product homogeneity} --- buyers cannot switch variety
\end{enumerate}
\end{column}

\begin{column}{0.45\linewidth}
\textbf{OPEC as reference case:}

OPEC controls \textasciitilde38\% of world oil supply. Oil: inelastic
demand, few substitutes, storable, high entry barriers.

OPEC's production cuts (2022--23) raised Brent crude from \$75 to
\$120/barrel.

\textbf{Agricultural cartels rarely succeed:}

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  Many producers (Brazil/USA/EU/India all produce wheat)
\item
  Many substitutes (rice ↔ wheat ↔ maize ↔ cassava)
\item
  Perishable goods --- cannot store to create scarcity
\item
  Low entry barriers --- new producers emerge when prices rise
\item
  Political pressure: domestic food security forces members to sell
\end{itemize}

\[\Rightarrow \text{Agricultural cartels are theoretically possible, practically unstable}\]
\end{column}
\end{columns}
\end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
\begin{block}{International Commodity Agreements: Instruments}
\phantomsection\label{international-commodity-agreements-instruments}
\begin{columns}[T]
\begin{column}{0.55\linewidth}
\textbf{ICAs} --- multilateral agreements between producing and
consuming countries to stabilise prices.

\textbf{Instruments used:}

\begin{longtable}[]{@{}
  >{\raggedright\arraybackslash}p{(\linewidth - 2\tabcolsep) * \real{0.5000}}
  >{\raggedright\arraybackslash}p{(\linewidth - 2\tabcolsep) * \real{0.5000}}@{}}
\toprule\noalign{}
\begin{minipage}[b]{\linewidth}\raggedright
Type
\end{minipage} & \begin{minipage}[b]{\linewidth}\raggedright
Mechanism
\end{minipage} \\
\midrule\noalign{}
\endhead
\textbf{Buffer stock} & Buy when \(P < P_{floor}\); sell when
\(P > P_{ceiling}\) \\
\textbf{Export quota} & Allocate export shares to members \\
\textbf{Multilateral contract} & Guaranteed volumes at agreed prices \\
\bottomrule\noalign{}
\end{longtable}

\textbf{Conditions for ICA success:}

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  Large share of world trade covered by members
\item
  Homogeneous commodity (easy to monitor)
\item
  Adequate buffer stock financing
\item
  Commitment from both producing and consuming countries
\item
  No close substitutes
\end{itemize}
\end{column}

\begin{column}{0.45\linewidth}
\textbf{Why ICAs fail:}

\begin{enumerate}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Free-rider:} non-members export outside quota at high prices
\item
  \textbf{Cheating:} members secretly exceed quotas
\item
  \textbf{Buffer stock cost:} storing perishable agri-commodities =
  enormous cost
\item
  \textbf{Technological change:} substitutes undermine demand (e.g.,
  synthetic rubber killed NR cartel)
\item
  \textbf{North-South conflict:} producers want high prices; consumers
  want low prices → negotiated price range always contested
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{UNCTAD's Integrated Programme for Commodities (1976):} ambitious
proposal for 18 ICAs --- most collapsed within 15 years.
\end{column}
\end{columns}
\end{block}

\begin{block}{History of International Commodity Agreements}
\phantomsection\label{history-of-international-commodity-agreements}
\begin{columns}[T]
\begin{column}{0.55\linewidth}
\textbf{Historical ICAs and their fate:}

\begin{longtable}[]{@{}lll@{}}
\toprule\noalign{}
Agreement & Commodity & Outcome \\
\midrule\noalign{}
\endhead
ICO & Coffee & Collapsed 1989 (free-rider) \\

\bottomrule\noalign{}
\end{longtable}
\end{column}

\begin{column}{0.45\linewidth}
\textbf{The tin crisis (1985) --- cautionary tale:}

The International Tin Agreement maintained a buffer stock. By 1985,
prices fell sharply. The buffer stock manager ran out of money --- could
no longer buy tin. The ITA collapsed overnight, taking with it the
London Metal Exchange tin market. Prices plunged 40\% in days.

\textbf{Lesson:} Buffer stocks require unlimited financing or credible
price bands. Neither is politically sustainable.
\end{column}
\end{columns}
\end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
\begin{block}{India's Rice Export Ban (2023): Unilateral Market Power}
\phantomsection\label{indias-rice-export-ban-2023-unilateral-market-power}
\begin{columns}[T]
\begin{column}{0.55\linewidth}
\textbf{India's rice export market position:}

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  World's largest rice exporter: \textbf{\textasciitilde40\% of global
  rice trade} (FY2022-23: 22 MMT)
\item
  Dominant in non-basmati white rice: \textasciitilde60\% of world
  market
\end{itemize}

\textbf{August 2023 policy actions:}

\begin{enumerate}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Ban} on non-basmati white rice exports
\item
  \textbf{20\% export duty} on parboiled rice
\item
  \textbf{\$1,200/tonne MEP} on basmati rice
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Immediate market impact:}

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  World rice prices surged \textbf{20--30\%} in 2 months
\item
  Philippines, Indonesia, West Africa most affected
\item
  Bangladesh, Senegal faced acute food security risks
\end{itemize}
\end{column}

\begin{column}{0.45\linewidth}
\textbf{Economic analysis:}

With 40\% world market share, India has significant
\textbf{monopsonist/monopolist power} in rice --- analogous to a cartel
without formal coordination.

\textbf{India's justification:} WTO Article XI permits export
restrictions for food security.

\textbf{Counter-argument:} When a single country controls 40\% of world
supply, its ``domestic food security'' restriction \emph{is} a global
food security threat.

\[P_{\text{world}} \uparrow 25\% \Rightarrow \text{500M+ people in importing countries pay more for food}\]

\textbf{Lesson:} Market concentration creates policy power --- with
global externalities.
\end{column}
\end{columns}
\end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
\begin{block}{Strategic Trade Policy and Infant Industry Argument}
\phantomsection\label{strategic-trade-policy-and-infant-industry-argument}
\begin{columns}[T]
\begin{column}{0.55\linewidth}
\textbf{Infant industry argument} (Hamilton 1791; List 1841):

Temporary protection allows a new industry to: 1. Achieve economies of
scale 2. Move down the learning curve 3. Become internationally
competitive

\textbf{Learning-by-doing model:}

\[C_t = C_0 \cdot e^{-\lambda Q_t^{\text{cumulative}}}\]

where \(\lambda\) = learning rate. Production cost falls with cumulative
experience.

\textbf{Protection justified if:}

\[\sum_{t=0}^{T} \frac{\text{PS gain}_t - \text{CS loss}_t}{(1+r)^t} > 0\]

i.e., present value of future gains exceeds current protection costs.
\end{column}

\begin{column}{0.45\linewidth}
\textbf{India examples:}

\textbf{Edible oil (1970s-90s):} Heavy protection of domestic oilseed
processing. - Result: Yellow revolution (oilseed production rose) - But
productivity gains lagged; protection lasted too long

\textbf{Sugar industry:} - 100+ years of protection - Still high-cost
producer by global standards - Protection → no incentive to compete

\textbf{Counter-argument to infant industry:} If the learning curve is
genuinely valuable, private capital should finance it. Government
intervention needed only if: (a) capital market failure, or (b) positive
spillovers (externalities) that private firm cannot capture.

\[\text{Subsidy justified} \iff \text{market failure exists}\]
\end{column}
\end{columns}
\end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{column}{0.45\linewidth}
\textbf{Countervailing Duties (CVD):}

CVDs target foreign \emph{subsidies} (not dumping):

\[\text{CVD} = \text{amount of foreign subsidy}\]

Condition: subsidy is ``specific'' (to a firm/industry) AND causes
material injury.

\textbf{SCMA Agreement} governs CVDs.

India has faced CVDs primarily from USA on shrimp, steel, and
pharmaceutical exports.

India has used CVDs less frequently than AD duties --- but use is
growing as India targets subsidised Chinese and EU agricultural exports.
\end{column}

\begin{frame}
\begin{block}{Key Takeaways: Lecture 8}
\phantomsection\label{key-takeaways-lecture-8}
\textbf{1. Export subsidies} distort production and trade; harm
importing-country farmers; impose fiscal costs on the subsidising
government. Net welfare effect for exporting country:
\(\Delta W = -(b+d) < 0\).

\textbf{2. India's MSP + FCI procurement} creates implicit export
subsidies when procurement prices exceed world prices. Sugar WTO dispute
(DS579) shows WTO's limits in disciplining developing country support.

\textbf{3. Dumping} = selling below normal value. Formal condition:
\(P_x < P_d\) or \(P_x < AVC\). India is the world's largest AD user;
but Indian exporters also face spurious AD allegations that confuse
genuine comparative advantage with dumping.

\textbf{4. VERs} are strictly worse than equivalent tariffs for the
importing country --- quota rent is transferred abroad:
\(\Delta W_{\text{VER}} = -(b+c+d) < -(b+d) = \Delta W_{\text{tariff}}\).

\textbf{5. Agricultural cartels} fail because: many producers, many
substitutes, perishable goods, easy entry. But India's unilateral market
power in rice (40\% of world exports) achieves cartel-like effects
without formal coordination.

\textbf{6. WTO disciplines} have significant gaps --- particularly on
export restrictions, state trading, and developing-country input
subsidies. This creates ongoing North-South tensions.
\end{block}
