\begin{block}{Introduction: Why the BoP Matters}
\phantomsection\label{introduction-why-the-bop-matters}
\textbf{Key questions for today:}

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  How does a country ``pay'' for its imports?
\item
  What happens when a country consistently imports more than it exports?
\item
  How do capital flows balance the external accounts?
\item
  When does a BoP \emph{crisis} occur?
\end{itemize}

\textbf{BoP accounting identity:}

\[\underbrace{\text{CA}}_{\text{trade + income}} + \underbrace{\text{KA}}_{\text{transfers}} + \underbrace{\text{FA}}_{\text{capital flows}} + \underbrace{\Delta R}_{\text{reserve change}} = 0\]

A current account deficit \emph{must} be financed by capital/financial
inflows or by running down foreign exchange reserves. If neither is
available → \textbf{BoP crisis} (India 1991).

\note{\textbf{India's external sector at a glance (FY2024):}

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  Merchandise exports: \textbf{\$437 billion} (goods)
\item
  Merchandise imports: \textbf{\$677 billion} (goods)
\item
  Trade deficit: \textbf{-\$240 billion} (goods only)
\item
  Services surplus: \textbf{+\$163B}
\item
  Remittances: \textbf{+\$120B} (world's largest recipient)
\item
  Current account: \textbf{-\$23B (-0.7\% of GDP)}
\end{itemize}}
\end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
\begin{block}{Balance of Trade: The Basics}
\phantomsection\label{balance-of-trade-the-basics}
\begin{columns}[T]
\begin{column}{0.55\linewidth}
\textbf{Balance of Trade (BoT)} = Merchandise exports − Merchandise
imports

\[\text{BoT} = X_{\text{goods}} - M_{\text{goods}}\]

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  Positive BoT = \textbf{trade surplus} (exports \textgreater{} imports)
\item
  Negative BoT = \textbf{trade deficit} (imports \textgreater{} exports)
\end{itemize}

\textbf{India's BoT trend (USD billion):}

\begin{longtable}[]{@{}llll@{}}

\midrule\noalign{}

FY22 & 422 & 614 & \textbf{-190} \\
FY23 & 451 & 714 & \textbf{-264} \\
FY24 & 437 & 677 & \textbf{-238} \\
\bottomrule\noalign{}
\end{longtable}

India \textbf{always} runs a merchandise trade deficit --- driven by
oil, gold, and electronics imports.
\end{column}

\begin{column}{0.45\linewidth}
\textbf{Why India's trade deficit is structural:}

\begin{longtable}[]{@{}lll@{}}

\end{column}

\begin{frame}
\begin{block}{National Income Identity Approach to the Current Account}
\phantomsection\label{national-income-identity-approach-to-the-current-account}
\begin{columns}[T]
\begin{column}{0.58\linewidth}
\textbf{National Income and Product Accounts (NIPA):}

\[Y = C + I + G + (X - M)\]

Rearranging:

\[X - M = Y - (C + I + G) = Y - A\]

where \(A = C + I + G\) is \textbf{absorption} (total domestic
spending).

\textbf{Implication:}

\[\boxed{\text{Current Account} \approx Y - A}\]

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  \(\text{CA} > 0 \Leftrightarrow Y > A\): country produces more than it
  spends → \emph{net lender} to world
\item
  \(\text{CA} < 0 \Leftrightarrow Y < A\): country spends more than it
  produces → \emph{net borrower} from world
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Saving-investment identity:}

\[Y - A = Y - C - I - G = (Y - C - G) - I = S_{\text{private}} + S_{\text{govt}} - I\]

\[\boxed{\text{CA} = (S - I) + (T - G)}\]

CA deficit = private dis-saving OR investment boom OR government deficit
(or combination).
\end{column}

\begin{column}{0.42\linewidth}
\textbf{India's CA decomposition (FY2024):}

\[\text{CA} = (S - I) + (T - G)\]

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  Private saving rate: \textasciitilde31\% of GDP
\item
  Investment rate: \textasciitilde32\% of GDP → private
  \(S - I \approx -1\%\) GDP
\item
  Fiscal deficit: \textasciitilde5.1\% of GDP → \(T - G \approx -5.1\%\)
\item
  CA = \(-1\% + (-5.1\%) \approx -6\%\)?
\end{itemize}

But India's CA = only \(-0.7\%\) GDP!

\textbf{Why?} Remittances (\$120B) and services surplus (\$163B) add
back \textasciitilde9-10\% of GDP in non-merchandise credits.

\textbf{Lesson:} CA = S-I identity holds, but ``saving'' must include
remittance income and service exports.
\end{column}
\end{columns}
\end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
\begin{block}{Current and Capital Account Structure}
\phantomsection\label{current-and-capital-account-structure}
\begin{columns}[T]
\begin{column}{0.55\linewidth}
\textbf{Account 1: Current Account (CA)}

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  Merchandise trade (goods): exports (+), imports (-)
\item
  Services trade: software, tourism, BPO
\item
  Primary income: investment income (interest, dividends, profits) ---
  net
\item
  Secondary income: remittances, grants, transfers
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Account 2: Capital Account (KA)}

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  Capital transfers (debt forgiveness, migrant assets)
\item
  Acquisition/disposal of non-produced, non-financial assets
\item
  (Small for most countries including India)
\end{itemize}
\end{column}

\begin{column}{0.45\linewidth}
\textbf{Accounting rule:}

Every international transaction has \textbf{two entries} of equal and
opposite sign (double-entry bookkeeping):

\textbf{Example: India imports \$10M of machines:}

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  Current Account (imports): \(-10M\)
\item
  Financial Account (credit from foreign bank): \(+10M\)
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Example: Indian software firm receives \$5M:}

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  Current Account (services export): \(+5M\)
\item
  Financial Account (forex held abroad initially): \(-5M\)
\end{itemize}
\end{column}
\end{columns}
\end{block}

\begin{block}{Financial Account and BoP Identity}
\phantomsection\label{financial-account-and-bop-identity}
\end{block}

\begin{block}{The Balance of Payments (BoP): Accounts + Identity}
\phantomsection\label{the-balance-of-payments-bop-accounts-identity}
\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Current Account (CA):} goods, services, income, transfers
\item
  \textbf{Capital Account (KA):} capital transfers (typically small)
\item
  \textbf{Financial Account (FA):} FDI, FPI, loans, and reserve changes
\item
  \textbf{Accounting identity:}
  \(\text{CA} + \text{KA} + \text{FA} + \Delta R = 0\)
\item
  If a CA deficit cannot be financed → \textbf{BoP crisis} (India 1991)
\end{itemize}
\end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{block}{Official Reserves and RBI Intervention}
\phantomsection\label{official-reserves-and-rbi-intervention}
\begin{columns}[T]
\begin{column}{0.55\linewidth}
\textbf{Official Foreign Exchange Reserves} = foreign assets held by
central bank (RBI):

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  Foreign currency assets (mainly USD, EUR, GBP, JPY)
\item
  Gold
\item
  SDRs (IMF Special Drawing Rights)
\item
  Reserve tranche position at IMF
\end{itemize}

\textbf{India's reserves trajectory:}

\begin{longtable}[]{@{}lll@{}}
\toprule\noalign{}
Year & Reserves (USD B) & Import cover \\
\midrule\noalign{}
\endhead
FY1991 & 1.2 & 2 weeks \\
FY2000 & 38 & 8 months \\
FY2008 & 309 & 14 months \\
FY2020 & 477 & 12 months \\
FY2024 & 646 & 11 months \\
\bottomrule\noalign{}
\end{longtable}

\textbf{Adequacy benchmark:} 3 months of imports (IMF rule of thumb).
India holds \textgreater11 months.
\end{column}

\begin{column}{0.45\linewidth}
\textbf{RBI's intervention role:}

RBI buys/sells USD to manage the exchange rate:

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Buy USD} (sell INR) → prevents excessive appreciation, builds
  reserves
\item
  \textbf{Sell USD} (buy INR) → prevents excessive depreciation,
  depletes reserves
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Managed float:} India operates a ``managed float'' --- neither
fully fixed nor fully free.

RBI intervenes to: 1. Smooth volatility (not set a target level) 2.
Prevent disorderly market conditions 3. Maintain competitive export
pricing (keep INR from appreciating too fast)

\textbf{FY2024:} RBI was a net buyer of USD --- absorbing strong capital
inflows.
\end{column}
\end{columns}
\end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
\begin{block}{BoP Adjustment Mechanisms}
\phantomsection\label{bop-adjustment-mechanisms}
\begin{columns}[T]
\begin{column}{0.55\linewidth}
When a BoP deficit exists, three adjustment mechanisms operate:

\textbf{1. Price mechanism (exchange rate adjustment):}

Currency depreciates → exports cheaper, imports expensive → CA improves
(if M-L condition holds).

\textbf{2. Income/expenditure mechanism:}

CA deficit → domestic income falls (through multiplier) → absorption
\(A\) falls → CA improves.

\[\Delta M = m \cdot \Delta Y \quad \text{(marginal propensity to import)}\]

\textbf{3. Monetary mechanism:}

CA deficit → reserve outflow → money supply falls → domestic prices fall
→ exports competitive again (Hume's price-specie-flow mechanism).

Modern version: reserve loss tightens monetary conditions → interest
rates rise → capital inflows.
\end{column}

\begin{column}{0.45\linewidth}
\textbf{Policy instruments:}

\begin{longtable}[]{@{}lll@{}}
\toprule\noalign{}
Problem & Instrument & Channel \\
\midrule\noalign{}
\endhead
CA deficit & Devaluation & Price competitiveness \\
CA deficit & Expenditure reduction & Income mechanism \\
FA deficit & Interest rate rise & Capital inflows \\
Reserve crisis & IMF credit & Financing \\
\bottomrule\noalign{}
\end{longtable}

\textbf{Expenditure-switching vs Expenditure-reducing:}

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  \emph{Switching}: devaluation shifts demand from foreign to domestic
  goods
\item
  \emph{Reducing}: fiscal/monetary austerity reduces total absorption
\end{itemize}

Both often needed simultaneously --- as India learned in 1991.
\end{column}
\end{columns}
\end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
\begin{block}{Marshall-Lerner Condition: When Does Depreciation Help?}
\phantomsection\label{marshall-lerner-condition-when-does-depreciation-help}
\begin{columns}[T]
\begin{column}{0.58\linewidth}
\textbf{Question:} Does a currency depreciation always improve the trade
balance?

\textbf{Answer:} Only if demand is elastic enough.

\textbf{Derivation:}

Let exchange rate \(e\) = domestic currency per unit of foreign.
Depreciation means \(\Delta e > 0\).

Trade balance in domestic currency:

\[TB = P_X \cdot Q_X - e \cdot P_M^* \cdot Q_M\]

where \(P_M^*\) = foreign price of imports, \(Q_X, Q_M\) = quantities.

After depreciation, \(e \uparrow\): - Export price (foreign) falls →
\(Q_X \uparrow\) by \(|e_X| \cdot \frac{\Delta e}{e}\) - Import price
(domestic) rises → \(Q_M \downarrow\) by
\(|e_M| \cdot \frac{\Delta e}{e}\)

\textbf{Marshall-Lerner (M-L) condition:}

\[\boxed{|e_X| + |e_M| > 1}\]

If satisfied, depreciation \emph{improves} the trade balance. If
\(|e_X| + |e_M| < 1\), depreciation \emph{worsens} TB (short-run
typical).
\end{column}

\begin{column}{0.42\linewidth}
\textbf{Intuition:}

A 10\% depreciation:

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  Makes exports 10\% cheaper abroad → quantity effect (positive)
\item
  Makes imports 10\% expensive at home → quantity effect (positive for
  TB)
\item
  But also raises value of existing imports → value effect (negative for
  TB)
\end{itemize}

\textbf{M-L says:} quantity effects must outweigh value effect.

\textbf{Typical elasticity estimates (long-run):}

\begin{longtable}[]{@{}llll@{}}
\toprule\noalign{}
\(|e_X|\) & \(|e_M|\) & Sum & M-L satisfied? \\
\midrule\noalign{}
\endhead
India & 0.8--1.2 & 0.7--1.0 & \textasciitilde1.5--2.2 \\
Short-run & 0.3--0.5 & 0.2--0.4 & \textasciitilde0.5--0.9 \\
\bottomrule\noalign{}
\end{longtable}

Short-run inelasticity: contracts pre-set, adjustment takes time.
\end{column}
\end{columns}
\end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
\begin{block}{J-Curve: Mechanisms}
\phantomsection\label{j-curve-mechanisms}
\begin{columns}[T]
\begin{column}{0.55\linewidth}
\textbf{Why does TB worsen initially?}

\textbf{Phase I (0--3 months): Contracts and inelasticity}

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  Import/export contracts already written in foreign currency
\item
  Prices change, but quantities cannot adjust instantly
\item
  Rising import prices → import \emph{bill} rises (value effect
  \textgreater{} quantity effect)
\item
  M-L condition NOT satisfied in short run:
  \(|e_X^{SR}| + |e_M^{SR}| < 1\)
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Phase II (3--12 months): Gradual adjustment}

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  Firms begin adjusting: export more, seek import substitutes
\item
  Trade volumes start responding to price signals
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Phase III (12+ months): Full adjustment}

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  Contracts renegotiated
\item
  Production capacity adjusted
\item
  Consumers switch to domestic substitutes
\item
  M-L holds: \(|e_X^{LR}| + |e_M^{LR}| > 1\)
\end{itemize}
\end{column}

\begin{column}{0.45\linewidth}
\textbf{India's empirical evidence:}

INR depreciation episodes: - \textbf{1991:} 25\% devaluation → J-curve
lasted \textasciitilde2 years (severe structural rigidities) -
\textbf{2013 taper tantrum:} INR fell 20\% → TB improved within 12
months (better-integrated trade) - \textbf{2022 (post-Russia-Ukraine):}
INR weakened 10\% → limited TB improvement (oil price dominated)

\textbf{Agricultural trade J-curve:}

Agricultural exports show faster adjustment than industrial goods ---
lower contract lock-in for spot commodity trades.

But Indian farmers often price in INR and don't automatically capture
depreciation gains without better market linkages.
\end{column}
\end{columns}
\end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
\begin{block}{Absorption Approach: Policy Implications}
\phantomsection\label{absorption-approach-policy-implications}
\begin{columns}[T]
\begin{column}{0.55\linewidth}
\textbf{Absorption approach (Sidney Alexander, 1952):}

\[CA = Y - A\]

To improve the CA (reduce deficit), need:

\[\Delta CA = \Delta Y - \Delta A > 0\]

Either: 1. \textbf{Raise output} \(Y\): supply-side reforms,
productivity growth 2. \textbf{Reduce absorption} \(A\): cut \(C\),
\(I\), or \(G\) (expenditure reduction) 3. \textbf{Both} (typical IMF
conditionality)

\textbf{Devaluation alone is insufficient:}

If \(A\) rises as fast as \(Y\) (say, because higher export revenues are
spent on imports), CA does not improve.

\textbf{Sterilisation problem:} If devaluation raises domestic price
level (inflation), real \(A\) may not fall → M-L gains are inflated
away.
\end{column}

\begin{column}{0.45\linewidth}
\textbf{India 1991 adjustment:}

\[\Delta CA = \Delta Y - \Delta A\]

IMF conditionality required: 1. Fiscal deficit cut: \(\Delta G < 0\) →
reduces \(A\) 2. 25\% devaluation → switches \(A\) toward domestic goods
3. Trade liberalisation → improves efficiency of \(Y\)

\textbf{Result:} - CA went from -3.1\% GDP (FY91) to -1.0\% (FY93) -
Growth fell temporarily (-0.1\% in FY91-92) - Inflation spiked (13.7\%
in FY92)

Classic adjustment tradeoff: \textbf{external balance restored at cost
of internal balance} --- at least temporarily.
\end{column}
\end{columns}
\end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
\begin{block}{Key Takeaways: Lecture 9}
\phantomsection\label{key-takeaways-lecture-9}
\textbf{1. National income identity:}
\(\text{CA} = Y - A = (S - I) + (T - G)\). A CA deficit means the
country borrows from abroad --- financed by FA inflows or reserve
drawdown.

\textbf{2. BoP accounting:}
\(\text{CA} + \text{KA} + \text{FA} + \Delta R = 0\). The BoP always
balances --- surpluses and deficits are in \emph{individual accounts},
not the overall BoP.

\textbf{3. India's external position (FY2024):} CA = -0.7\% GDP
(comfortable); remittances = \$120B (world's largest); services surplus
= \$163B; forex reserves = \$646B (11 months imports). External position
is \textbf{strong}.

\textbf{4. Marshall-Lerner condition:} \(|e_X| + |e_M| > 1\) must hold
for depreciation to improve TB. In the short run, M-L is typically NOT
satisfied → J-curve: TB worsens before improving.

\textbf{5. India 1991 crisis:} Reserves fell to \$1.2B (2 weeks
imports); India pledged 67 tonnes gold to the BoE. IMF bailout triggered
liberalisation --- delicensing, trade reform, FDI opening. Crisis →
structural reform.

\note{\textbf{6. Agricultural BoP:} India runs a trade surplus in
agriculture (\textasciitilde\$20B), but edible oil imports (-\$21B) are
the single largest structural weakness. MSP policy affects both food
security and export competitiveness.}
