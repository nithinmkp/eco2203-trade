\begin{block}{Opening Reflection}
\phantomsection\label{opening-reflection}
\textbf{Eighteen weeks ago, we asked:}

\begin{quote}
\emph{Why do countries trade? And what does that mean for the 600
million Indians who depend on agriculture for their livelihoods?}
\end{quote}

Today, we can answer this with \textbf{theory + data + institutions +
policy}.
\end{block}

\begin{frame}
\begin{block}{The Full Chain: Course Synthesis Diagram}
\phantomsection\label{the-full-chain-course-synthesis-diagram}
\textbf{Course Synthesis: International Trade Logic Chain}

\begin{enumerate}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Comparative Advantage} → countries gain from specialisation
\item
  \textbf{Gains from Trade} → mediated by terms of trade
\item
  \textbf{Trade Policy} → tariffs, quotas, subsidies shape distribution
\item
  \textbf{BOP \& Exchange Rates} → macro context for trade flows
\item
  \textbf{International Institutions} → WTO, AoA, SPS discipline policy
\item
  \textbf{India's Strategy} → Export promotion + selective protection
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Every lecture has been one step in this chain.}

The central tension of the entire course:

{Free trade maximises efficiency} --- comparative advantage theory,
consumer gains, global welfare.

{But agriculture is not like other sectors} --- food is existential,
farming communities are politically powerful, and market failures
(information asymmetry, price volatility, public goods in R\&D) are
pervasive.

The discipline of agricultural trade economics is, fundamentally, the
study of how societies navigate this tension.
\end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
\begin{block}{India's agricultural trade (FY2024): headline numbers}
\phantomsection\label{indias-agricultural-trade-fy2024-headline-numbers}
\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Exports:} \$43.7B
\item
  \textbf{Imports:} \$28.2B
\item
  \textbf{Surplus:} \$15.5B
\item
  \textbf{Global rank:} \textasciitilde9th largest agri exporter (WTO)
\item
  Agriculture ≈ \textbf{11\%} of India's merchandise exports
\end{itemize}

FY2024 is lower than the FY2022 peak, but still among India's
highest-ever export years.
\end{block}

\begin{frame}
\begin{block}{Why FY2022 was a record year}
\phantomsection\label{why-fy2022-was-a-record-year}
\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  Global food commodity prices spiked post-pandemic
\item
  Russia--Ukraine war disrupted major suppliers
\item
  Strong basmati demand (Gulf)
\item
  Marine exports expanded (high aquaculture output)
\end{itemize}
\end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
\begin{block}{Why FY2024 fell from the peak}
\phantomsection\label{why-fy2024-fell-from-the-peak}
\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  Rice export restrictions tightened from 2023 (credibility + volumes)
\item
  Global commodity prices corrected from highs
\item
  Cotton profitability weakened (cost + pest pressure)
\end{itemize}
\end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
\begin{block}{Export composition (FY2024): where the dollars come from}
\phantomsection\label{export-composition-fy2024-where-the-dollars-come-from}
\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Marine products} (\textasciitilde17\%): India's single largest
  agri export category
\item
  \textbf{Non-basmati rice} (\textasciitilde15\%)
\item
  \textbf{Basmati rice} (\textasciitilde13\%)
\item
  \textbf{Processed foods} (\textasciitilde11\%)
\item
  \textbf{Spices} (\textasciitilde9\%)
\end{itemize}

Rice is \textasciitilde{}\textbf{28\%} of agri exports in FY2024 →
market power, but also concentration risk.
\end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
\begin{block}{Rice concentration: market power vs credibility}
\phantomsection\label{rice-concentration-market-power-vs-credibility}
\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  When India restricts rice exports, global prices can spike
\item
  Many countries rely on Indian rice for food security
\item
  Export controls create \textbf{credibility risk} for India as a
  supplier
\item
  Export earnings become more volatile (policy shocks)
\end{itemize}
\end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
\begin{block}{Case: India--UAE CEPA (May 2022)}
\phantomsection\label{case-indiauae-cepa-may-2022}
What CEPA did (agriculture examples):

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  Preferential tariffs for basmati, seafood, fruits, and processed foods
  (select lines)
\item
  Early outcome: India--UAE agri trade up \textasciitilde28\% (FY2023)
\end{itemize}

Takeaway: FTAs with meaningful agricultural chapters can raise market
access and incentivise quality upgrading.
\end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
\begin{block}{Import Profile: India's Agricultural Dependencies}
\phantomsection\label{import-profile-indias-agricultural-dependencies}
India's biggest agricultural import exposure is \textbf{edible oils}.

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  Edible oils (palm + soy + sunflower): \textasciitilde\$14B+ annual
  bill; \textasciitilde60--65\% of consumption imported
\item
  Pulses: \textasciitilde\$2--3B; key sources include Canada, Myanmar,
  Australia
\item
  Other items (fruits, cashew, spices) are smaller and less
  macro-critical
\end{itemize}

Key point: edible oils are the single largest driver of India's agri
import vulnerability.

\note{FY2024 headline agri imports: \textasciitilde\$28B (approx). Main
suppliers: Indonesia/Malaysia (palm), Argentina/Brazil (soy),
Ukraine/Russia (sunflower). Without the edible oil drag, the agri trade
surplus would be much larger.}
\end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
\begin{block}{Why edible oil dependence is a structural vulnerability}
\phantomsection\label{why-edible-oil-dependence-is-a-structural-vulnerability}
\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  Household inflation sensitivity: global edible oil prices pass through
  quickly
\item
  External vulnerability: export bans and war shocks can create sudden
  shortages
\item
  Persistent gap: demand growth has outpaced domestic oilseed
  productivity
\end{itemize}
\end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
\begin{block}{Policy response: NMEO--OP (2021)}
\phantomsection\label{policy-response-nmeoop-2021}
\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  Goal: expand oil palm and raise domestic edible oil output
\item
  Constraints: land availability, 3--4 year gestation, crop-choice
  incentives
\item
  Implication: dependence likely remains a medium-run constraint even
  with policy push
\end{itemize}
\end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
\begin{block}{The Value Addition Opportunity}
\phantomsection\label{the-value-addition-opportunity}
India often exports \textbf{raw / semi-processed} products. Value
addition raises earnings per tonne.

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  Value addition = processing + branding + standards + cold chain
\item
  Big gains are in processed foods, nutraceuticals, and ready-to-cook
  formats
\item
  Binding constraints are typically quality compliance, scale, and
  logistics
\end{itemize}
\end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
\begin{block}{Example 1: Cashew (raw vs processed)}
\phantomsection\label{example-1-cashew-raw-vs-processed}
\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  India produces raw cashew, but much processing happens abroad
\item
  Processed grades + packaging command multiples of the raw price
\item
  Domestic processing = jobs + higher export value + brand ownership
\end{itemize}
\end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
\begin{block}{Example 2: Turmeric (raw vs extracts)}
\phantomsection\label{example-2-turmeric-raw-vs-extracts}
\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  Raw turmeric is a bulk commodity
\item
  Extracts/curcumin and finished supplements capture most of the margin
\item
  Needs certification, R\&D, and consistent quality
\end{itemize}
\end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
\begin{block}{Example 3: Shrimp (commodity vs value-added)}
\phantomsection\label{example-3-shrimp-commodity-vs-value-added}
\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  Commodity exports: raw frozen shrimp
\item
  Value-added: breaded / marinated / ready-to-cook formats (price
  premium)
\item
  Requires cold chain, HACCP compliance, and processing capacity
\end{itemize}
\end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
\begin{block}{FTP 2023--28: what it implies for value addition}
\phantomsection\label{ftp-202328-what-it-implies-for-value-addition}
\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  Prioritise processed food and RTE/RCC categories
\item
  Build compliance: FSSAI-aligned plants, traceability, lab testing
\item
  Use branding tools: ODOP + GI where appropriate
\end{itemize}

\note{Keep Mega Food Parks / PM-FME details for oral discussion.}
\end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
\begin{block}{Foreign Trade Policy (FTP) 2023--2028: overview}
\phantomsection\label{foreign-trade-policy-ftp-20232028-overview}
\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  Released \textbf{March 31, 2023} (Ministry of Commerce)
\item
  Vision: total exports (goods + services) reach \textbf{\$2T by 2030}
\item
  Shift: from export subsidies → \textbf{tax remission} (RoDTEP)
\item
  Emphasis: collaboration + paperless, faster processes
\item
  New: explicit \textbf{e-commerce export} push (target \textbf{\$200B}
  by 2030)
\end{itemize}
\end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
\begin{block}{FTP 2023--2028: what matters for agriculture}
\phantomsection\label{ftp-20232028-what-matters-for-agriculture}
\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Districts as Export Hubs (DEH)} (district-level export plans)
\item
  \textbf{ODOP} branding for district ``signature'' products
\item
  \textbf{Millets + organic} positioned as emerging export areas
\item
  \textbf{GI promotion} to earn price premia and protect brands
\item
  \textbf{TMA + APEDA expansion} to support logistics and market access
\end{itemize}

\note{Amnesty scheme: one-time settlement for exporters with unresolved
EPCG/Advance Authorisation obligations.}
\end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
\begin{block}{Core policy dilemma (1): MSP vs export competitiveness}
\phantomsection\label{core-policy-dilemma-1-msp-vs-export-competitiveness}
\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  Higher \textbf{MSP} raises farm incomes but can make exports
  uncompetitive
\item
  Example (wheat, 2023--24): MSP \textbf{₹2,275/quintal
  (\textasciitilde\$274/MT)} vs world price
  \textbf{\textasciitilde\$220/MT}
\item
  If exports still happen, it often requires \textbf{subsidies} or
  special channels
\item
  Political economy: farm support is sticky; export markets are
  price-sensitive
\item
  Result: policy must balance \textbf{income support} and \textbf{market
  access}
\end{itemize}
\end{block}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
\begin{block}{Key Takeaways for the Examination and for Life}
\phantomsection\label{key-takeaways-for-the-examination-and-for-life}
\textbf{Five messages to carry beyond this classroom:}

\begin{enumerate}
\item
  \textbf{{Comparative advantage, not absolute advantage, drives
  specialisation}} --- India need not be the cheapest in everything; it
  only needs a relative cost advantage in what it exports. This is
  Ricardo's enduring insight.
\item
  \textbf{{Free trade maximises efficiency, but food security requires
  nuanced policy}} --- the tension between these goals is real,
  legitimate, and never fully resolved. The best economists acknowledge
  the trade-offs rather than pretending they do not exist.
\item
  \textbf{{WTO rules constrain AND protect India's agricultural policy
  space}} --- India must simultaneously defend its right to support
  farmers and work to ensure global rules are fair for developing
  country agriculture. Both are valid positions.
\item
  \textbf{{SPS compliance is the critical bottleneck for India's agri
  export growth}} --- not tariffs, not logistics, not finance. The
  moment India's spice, seafood, or fresh produce consistently meets the
  safety standards of the EU, USA, and Japan, billions of dollars of
  additional export value become accessible.
\item
  \textbf{{Value addition, not raw commodity exports, is the path to
  \$100 billion}} --- India must move from exporting raw cashew to
  exporting cashew butter; from raw turmeric to curcumin capsules; from
  frozen shrimp to ready-to-cook seafood meals. That transition is the
  work of the next decade.
\end{enumerate}
\end{block}
\end{frame}
